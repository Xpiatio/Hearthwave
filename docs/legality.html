GMRS · FCC Part 95, Subpart E

Legal by architecture,not by promise.

Hearthwave is a remote control point for one GMRS station you own and license — a keyboard, screen, and speaker for the radio in your house. The FCC explicitly permits that. What it prohibits — internet-linked repeaters, phone patches, retransmission between stations — Hearthwave cannot do, because the code to do it does not exist. This page walks through the rules and shows exactly how the design maps onto them.

47 CFR §§ 95.1745 · 95.1749

It's remote control — expressly permitted

The rules state that GMRS base stations "may be operated by remote control," and may be connected to "the public switched network or other networks for the sole purpose of operation by remote control." Your browser is the control point; the station is the radio.

47 CFR § 95.1705(c)

You control who reaches the hub

Accounts exist only if the admin — the licensee — creates them. Passwords, session tokens, a transmit-blocking listen-only mode, and a per-user audit log keep station access inside the household your license covers.

47 CFR § 95.1733(a)(8)

No internet repeater linking

What the FCC actually prohibits is using a network to link stations and extend radio range. Hearthwave has no linking, no repeater bridge, no voice-over-IP interconnect — received audio becomes text on your screen and never touches another transmitter.

The architecture

One station. One radio. One control link.

Hearthwave never generates a radio signal. It synthesizes speech through a sound card wired to your own Part 95E–certified radio and keys the push-to-talk line — the same thing a microphone and a finger do, reachable from a browser. Everything on the network side is control traffic for that one station: typed text in, transcribed text out.

The path that does not exist: radio → internet → another radio. There is no code in Hearthwave that carries received audio to another transmitter, another repeater, another Hearthwave instance, or a telephone. Incoming transmissions become text on a screen — a family message log — and stop there.

Every rule, answered in code.

Every transmission is written to an audit log with the account, callsign, and message text; net sessions are journaled with rosters and transcripts. If a question is ever raised, the licensee can show who transmitted what, and when.
Full disclosure

Automated functions, named and bounded.

A credible compliance page names its edge cases. Nearly everything Hearthwave transmits is one human action → one transmission. Four functions can key the radio on a timer or trigger instead — here is each one, what gates it, and what it asks of you. Note that Part 95E permits automatic (unattended) control only for repeater stations (§ 95.1747); a Hearthwave base station is designed to be run attended — these functions assist an operator, they don't replace one.

Automatic station ID

ALWAYS ON

Transmits "This is <callsign>" when 15 minutes elapse during station activity.

  • ✦Why it exists: it is § 95.1751 — the identification the rule demands, on the rule's own schedule.
  • ✦Gating: only fires after the station has actually transmitted; silent stations stay silent.

Monitoring beacon

OFF BY DEFAULT

Optionally announces "<callsign> Hearthwave base, monitoring." at a set interval (default 15 minutes).

  • ✦Gating: disabled unless the admin turns it on; skipped whenever the channel is busy; leads with the callsign, so it also satisfies the ID rule.
  • ✦Your duty: enable it only while the station is genuinely attended and monitoring — the message should be true when it airs. One-way announcements on GMRS are limited (§ 95.1733(b)); keep this as a brief availability call, which is what it is.

Net announcement (NCS)

ACTIVE NET ONLY

While a net-control session is running, periodically announces the net and invites check-ins.

  • ✦Gating: exists only while an operator has deliberately opened a net and is running it; waits for a clear channel; stops when the net closes.
  • ✦Your duty: the net-control operator stays at the position for the duration of the net — the announcement is the NCS's voice, not a substitute for the NCS.

SKYWARN alert relay (NCS)

ACTIVE NET + ZONE SET

During an active weather net, reads new National Weather Service Extreme/Severe alerts for your configured county zone over the air.

  • ✦Gating: requires an operator-run net and a deliberately configured NWS zone; only the most severe alert classes air; waits for a clear channel.
  • ✦Why it's defensible: emergency and safety-of-life communications are GMRS's highest-priority permitted use (§ 95.1731(a)) — and the supervising net-control operator is present the entire time it can run.
The other half of legal

What the software can't do for you.

Hearthwave keeps the station inside the rules. Keeping the operation inside the rules is the licensee's job — the same job every GMRS operator has, with or without software. The short list:

  • ✓
    Hold a GMRS licenseOne license covers your immediate family (§ 95.1705). No license, no transmitting — use listen-only accounts until yours is granted.
  • ✓
    Use a Part 95E–certified radioHearthwave is the control point; the certified radio is the station. Don't wire it to equipment not certified for GMRS.
  • ✓
    Keep accounts inside the licenseCreate transmit-capable accounts only for immediate family covered by your license; give everyone else listen-only. You are responsible for every operator, including minors (§ 95.1743).
  • ✓
    Keep the hub off the public internetHearthwave ships no relay, tunnel, or port-forwarding service on purpose. Away from home, reach it over your own VPN (WireGuard/Tailscale) — the control link stays yours, exactly as § 95.1749 intends.
  • ✓
    Stay present for automated functionsEnable the beacon only when the station is attended; run nets from the chair, not from memory.
  • ✓
    Transmit permissible contentPlain-language personal communications (§ 95.1731). No music, ads, coded messages, or false traffic (§ 95.1733(a)) — software can frame your callsign, but what you say is on you.
